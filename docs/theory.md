# Theory notes

This document collects the theoretical background that motivates the
implementation choices in `eml_gam/` and the experiments in
`eml_gam/benchmarks/`. The notes are self-contained at the level of a
workshop or conference appendix. Sections on convergence and
sample-complexity are sketched rather than proven; rigorous proofs are
left as pointers for follow-up work.

The primary reference for the operator itself is Odrzywolek, 2026
(arXiv:2603.21852). The primary reference for the additive structure is
Lou, Caruana, Gehrke, Hooker, KDD 2013 (GA^2M / EBM).

## 1. Notation and setup

Let `x in R^d` denote a feature vector. A depth-`n` EML tree is a map
`T : R^(n_inputs) -> R` of the form

    T(x) = eml(s_0, s_1),      eml(a, b) = exp(a) - log(b),

where `s_0, s_1` are themselves selected from the set

    {1, x_1, ..., x_{n_inputs}, f_child(x)}

via a softmax over learnable logits, and `f_child` is the output of a
depth-(n-1) subtree. At the bottom level (`depth - 1`) the `f_child`
option is removed, leaving only the constant `1` and the input
variables. The parameter counts (see `test_param_counts_univariate`
and `test_param_counts_bivariate`) are

    n_params_univariate(n) = 5 * 2**n - 6,
    n_params_bivariate(n)  = 7 * 2**n - 8.

An input affine transform `x -> scale * x + offset` is optional (flag
`use_input_affine`). The surrounding GA^2M wraps each tree in a scalar
output weight and sums main-effect and pairwise-interaction trees over
a bias.

## 2. Expressivity of EML

Odrzywolek's Theorem 1 shows that every primitive in a standard
"scientific-calculator" set (including `exp`, `log`, `+, -, *, /`, the
trig and hyperbolic families, `pi`, `e`, `i`, `-1`) is representable by
a finite composition of `eml` over `{1, x, y}`. The proof is constructive
(Table 4 of the paper gives explicit rewrites). The depth required by
the prototype EML-compiler is unbounded; direct exhaustive search gives
tighter bounds such as `K = 11` for `x - y` and `K = 17` for `x * y`.

For the practical regressor implemented here we care about what can be
represented at **finite small depth**. The default `univariate_depth = 2`
representation spans a 14-entry atlas (see `primitives.univariate_atlas_depth2`)
which includes `exp`, `log`, `1/x`, `e - log(x)`, `exp(exp(x))`, and
nine more. Depth 3 covers deeper compositions but — as Section 3 of this
document and the landscape benchmark demonstrate — training from random
initialisation becomes unreliable at depth 3 and collapses at depth 5.

### 2.1 Identifiability and effective degrees of freedom

After the final `argmax` snap, a univariate depth-`n` tree in an EMLGAM
has

* 0 softmax logits (all frozen by the snap),
* `2 * n_inputs` affine parameters (scale, offset) when
  `use_input_affine=True`,
* 1 scalar output weight (lives on the outer GA^2M).

So the post-snap degrees of freedom per tree is `2 * n_inputs + 1`. Some
of these are redundant for specific snaps: for example the snap
`exp(x) = eml(x, 1)` admits the rescaling `w * exp(scale * x + offset) =
w * exp(offset) * exp(scale * x)`, so offset and output weight are
degenerate. The `canonical form` we adopt when printing formulas resolves
the redundancy by keeping the output weight separate and leaving
`offset` free; identifiability is therefore up to this affine
absorption, which is made explicit in `param_summary`.

We do **not** currently enforce identifiability during training — the
redundancy never hurts MSE, and the Hessian-based uncertainty quantifiers
we provide are reported per formula rather than per parameter. A clean
algebraic canonicalisation step is an obvious follow-up.

## 3. Optimisation landscape

The central empirical observation of Odrzywolek's paper — and the main
scientific contribution of **this** repository — is that gradient-based
recovery of EML trees suffers a sharp transition in depth:

| init mode     | d=1 | d=2  | d=3 | d=4 | d=5 |
|---------------|-----|------|-----|-----|-----|
| random        | 100 | 3.3  | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.0 |
| perturb sigma=0.5 | 100 | 100  | 100 | 100 | 100 |
| perturb sigma=1.0 | 100 | 100  | 100 | 100 | 100 |
| perturb sigma=2.0 | 100 | 100  | 86.7 | 86.7 | 66.7 |
| perturb sigma=5.0 | 100 | 53.3 | 53.3 | 6.7 | 6.7 |

(Source: `landscape_results.json`, generated by
`eml_gam/benchmarks/landscape.py`. Success = final MSE below `1e-3` over
30 random-init / 15 perturbed-init trials per cell.)

Two regimes are visible:

* A **random-init wall**: from `d = 3` onward, random initialisation
  essentially never recovers the target. This reproduces Odrzywolek's
  Figure 4 observation but on a harder set of targets (`e - exp^{d-2}(x)`,
  chosen to be non-degenerate across depths).
* A **basin radius that shrinks with depth**: perturbation experiments
  with known good snaps show that the correct basin has effective
  radius (in logit space) of `~1` at depth 2 and `~0.5` at depth 5.
  When the perturbation exceeds the basin radius we still see the
  optimiser reaching low MSE, but the **recovered snap** is no longer
  the ground-truth one — the tree settles in a distinct basin that
  encodes a different symbolic formula with an indistinguishable
  numerical fit.

The gap between the `low_mse` and `snap_match` columns of the landscape
report therefore quantifies the density of alternative local minima.
That gap widens monotonically with depth: at `d = 2, sigma = 2.0` we
see 100 per cent MSE recovery and 80 per cent snap match; at
`d = 4, sigma = 2.0` it is 86.7 per cent and 6.7 per cent respectively.
This is — to our knowledge — the first quantitative demonstration of
symbolic degeneracy in EML space.

### 3.1 Practical consequences

The collapse motivates three of the algorithmic choices in the library:

* **Primitive-atlas warm-start.** Section 4 of Odrzywolek's paper notes
  that "local perturbation of correct weights recovers 100 per cent
  even at depth 6." The `primitives.warm_start_tree` routine provides
  exactly that: rather than starting from a random point in logit
  space, it installs a known-good snap (picked from a hand-coded atlas
  by holdout-R^2 projection onto the target residual) and perturbs
  around it. This side-steps the landscape collapse for as long as the
  atlas covers the target class.
* **Adaptive multi-start with atlas rotation.** When the first-ranked
  atlas entry does not match the true target, the multi-start wrapper
  rotates through the top-`k` candidates on successive restarts instead
  of re-picking the same deterministic winner. See
  `primitives.rank_atlas_candidates` and the rank-parameter plumbing
  in `EMLGAM._warm_start_trees`.
* **NaN-abort + extrapolation penalty.** Deep EML trees can produce
  NaN gradients when nested `exp` arguments cross the clamp boundary.
  The training loop detects this and aborts cleanly so the multi-start
  wrapper can move on. A soft Lipschitz-style penalty optionally
  discourages snaps that explode just outside the training box, which
  eliminates the seed-dependent divergence on `competitive_inhibition`
  observed in the original readme (see
  `benchmarks/scientific.py::competitive_inhibition` and the
  `extrap_penalty_weight` field of `TrainConfig`).

### 3.2 A smooth surrogate operator

Odrzywolek's "Open problems" section explicitly calls for a binary
Sheffer-like operator without the composed-exponential asymptotics that
drive the clamp pathology. The `sheffer` module contains a candidate:

    psi(x, y) = sinh(x) - arsinh(y).

`sinh` is asymptotically `e^x / 2` in the upper tail and `-e^{-x} / 2`
in the lower tail, but smooth at zero; `arsinh` is smooth on the entire
real line and requires no clamping even for negative arguments. The
stability benchmark in `benchmarks/sheffer_stability.py` trains
identically parameterised `eml` and `psi` trees on simple targets from
random init and measures final MSE. The result at depth 2-4 is nuanced:
`psi` is **more stable** (no NaN events), but **less accurate** on
targets of depth `<=` tree depth because its exponential tail is half
that of `exp`. Expressivity parity of `psi` with `eml` in the deep-tree
limit is conjectured rather than proven; it is an obvious target for
follow-up theoretical work.

## 4. Warm-start and the two-sided holdout scorer

Given an atlas entry `phi(x)` we score the projection
`alpha + beta * phi(x) ~= y` by holdout R^2 on the outer edges of the
first feature's range. Concretely:

1. Sort the samples by the first input feature.
2. Reserve the outer `5 per cent` on each side for validation; train on
   the central `80 per cent`.
3. Fit `(alpha, beta)` by ordinary least squares on the training slice.
4. Report the `R^2` of the predicted `alpha + beta * phi(x)` on the
   union of the two validation edges.

This biases the atlas ranker toward primitives with the correct
**asymptotic** behaviour, which is what matters for extrapolation. A
primitive like `1/x` gives fantastic in-sample `R^2` on a Michaelis-Menten
target, but catastrophic validation `R^2` because the large-`x` and
small-`x` tails behave completely differently. The outlier-ratio and
dynamic-range guards reject such pathological candidates outright.

An adaptive simplicity tolerance breaks near-ties in favour of earlier
atlas entries (which, by convention, are the simpler ones). The
tolerance is computed as
`max(0.05, min(simplicity_tol, (1 - best_R^2) * 0.5))` — it shrinks when
the best candidate already fits almost perfectly and opens up when the
best candidate is mediocre, which is when simplicity pays off most.

## 5. Effective-DoF reporting

`EMLGAM.param_summary()` returns a dictionary with three counts relevant
for complexity-adjusted comparisons (e.g. Akaike information, Pareto
fronts in SRBench):

* `n_raw_logits`: total softmax logits across all trees. These vanish
  after snap but contribute to capacity during training.
* `n_slot_choices`: integer-valued slot DoF after snap. Useful for
  Bayesian model-selection arguments that treat snap choices as
  structural DoF (one bit per choice, with appropriate prior).
* `n_continuous_post_snap`: the continuous parameters that remain in
  the live model at inference: per-input scale and offset, one scalar
  output weight per tree, plus the bias.

For a GA^2M with `d = 3` features, `univariate_depth = 2`, and two
interaction pairs, the post-snap continuous DoF is `3 * 3 + 2 * 5 + 1 =
20`. Compare to an EBM of comparable accuracy which typically uses tens
of splines per feature (hundreds of DoF). EML-GA^2M's structural
parsimony is a direct consequence of the `argmax` snap, which trades
continuous capacity for symbolic structure.

## 6. Limitations and open directions

The empirical contributions of this repository (robust stabilisation of
a previously fragile training loop, the landscape basin-width
quantification, and a smooth-Sheffer candidate operator) establish
**what is currently possible** but do not answer any of the deeper
theoretical questions:

* A convergence proof for the soft-to-hard annealing schedule would
  formalise the assumption that the final snap is optimal within the
  winning basin. Existing Gumbel-softmax analyses (Jang et al. 2017;
  Maddison et al. 2017) are suggestive but do not directly apply to
  our discrete-then-continuous trajectory.
* Approximation rates as a function of tree depth for a given elementary
  function class are unknown. Yarotsky-style rates for ReLU nets give a
  template; applying it to EML trees would likely require a careful
  characterisation of the "Taylor-like" recursion around the identity
  primitive `e = eml(1, 1)`.
* Sample complexity for **structural** recovery is open. The MSE-good,
  snap-wrong regime observed in Section 3 means MSE-consistent
  estimation does not imply symbolic consistency; a separate
  information-theoretic argument (counting the Catalan number of
  depth-`n` trees) is needed.

These are good NeurIPS / ICML follow-up targets and are not attempted
here.
